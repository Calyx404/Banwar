The Story Behind Banwar

Born From an 18th-Century Chronicle of Igorot Life

Banwar is rooted in the writings of Francisco Antolin, a Spanish missionary who explored the Cordillera in the mid-1700s. His accounts describe Igorot communities, bustling gold and copper mines, and encounters with colonial expeditions. Translated by historian William Henry Scott, this rare chronicle offers one of the earliest windows into Cordilleran life. We transformed these historical descriptions into digital points, linking written memory to living geography.

Notices of the Pagan Igorots in the Interior of the Island of Manila

ABOUT THE BOOK

“Notices of the Pagan Igorots in the interior of the Island of Manila” is an 18th-century ethnographic account of the indigenous Igorot peoples of northern Luzon, wirrten by Francisco Antolín in 1789. The manuscript documents Igorot society, culture, and economic activities, particualrly their gold and copper mining operations.

It captures these highland communities during Spanish colonial influence and serves as one of the few contemporary colonial accounts focusing specifically on the Igorot peoples. The work has become an essential primary source for historians and anthropologists studying pre-modern Philippine societies.

Francisco Antolín

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Francisco Antolín was a Spanish Dominican friar who served as Provincial historian and missionary in the Philippines during the late 18th century. He combined his religious duties with scholarly pursuits, dedicating his career to documenting the indigenous peoples of the Philippine archipelago.

Antolín completed his work on the Igorots in summer 1789, demonstrating his commitment to preserving knowledge about indigenous communities during colonial change. He died in 1796, leaving behind valuable documentary evidence that continues to be referenced by modern scholars.

This project is more than coursework—it is a collaborative effort to preserve and share the Cordillera's heritage with the world.

Project Banwar

Journey Into the Cordillera's Living History

What if the mountains could tell their stories? Banwar maps places described in an 18th-century Spanish chronicle that documented the Cordillera region in Northern Philippines—home to resilient communities, thriving trade, and untold encounters. We transformed these writings into digital markers you can freely explore. Click, navigate, and let the past unfold like never before.

Explore Our Features

Trace Centuries of Stories Through a Living Map

Banwar turns historical research into an interactive journey. Navigate through a digital map filled with places mentioned in centuries-old texts. Search using their original names and watch history appear right where it happened. Export clean PNG or PDF maps for your study, teaching, or personal exploration. It is history made easy to explore.

The Story Behind Banwar

Born From an 18th-Century Chronicle of Igorot Life

Banwar is rooted in the writings of Francisco Antolin, a Spanish missionary who explored the Cordillera in the mid-1700s. His accounts describe Igorot communities, bustling gold and copper mines, and encounters with colonial expeditions. Translated by historian William Henry Scott, this rare chronicle offers one of the earliest windows into Cordilleran life. We transformed these historical descriptions into digital points, linking written memory to living geography.

Who Made This Possible

Proud Igorot Students Mapping Cultural Heritage

Banwar was created by Information Technology students from the College of Information Sciences, Benguet State University. Through research, design, and code, we built a digital bridge between history and technology. This project is more than coursework—it is a collaborative effort to preserve and share the Cordillera's heritage with the world.
